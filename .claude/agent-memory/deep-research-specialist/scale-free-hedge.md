**Original claim (Act XX, correctly cited)**: Barabási, A.-L., & Albert, R. (1999). Emergence of scaling in random networks. *Science*, 286(5439), 509-512. Introduced preferential attachment as a generative mechanism producing power-law degree distributions.

**The hedge**: The strong claim that "most real networks are scale-free" has been substantially challenged.

**Primary critique**: Broido, A. D., & Clauset, A. (2019). Scale-free networks are rare. *Nature Communications*, 10, 1017. Analysed ~1000 networks and found only ~4% showed strong evidence of scale-free structure; many real networks have heavier or lighter tails than pure power-laws.

**Practical implication for the story**: The narrative ("preferential attachment is the gravity of networks") still works as a *tendency* — hubs do form, rich-get-richer dynamics are real. But the language "most real networks... grow through preferential attachment" is stronger than current evidence supports.

**Why:** A network scientist reading Act XX will know about the Broido-Clauset critique. The story doesn't need to debate it — just soften "most" to "many" or "a wide class of."

**How to apply:** If revising Act XX, change "most real networks — the web, citation graphs, social systems — grow through preferential attachment" to something like "many real networks show preferential attachment dynamics: new nodes are far more likely to connect to nodes that already have many connections." Preserves the narrative without inviting the universality challenge.
