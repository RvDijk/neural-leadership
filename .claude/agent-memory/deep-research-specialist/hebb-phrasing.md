"Cells that fire together, wire together" is **not** Hebb's wording. It was coined by Carla Shatz in: Shatz, C. J. (1992). The developing brain. *Scientific American*, 267(3), 60-67.

Hebb's actual 1949 formulation (*The Organization of Behavior*, p.62): "When an axon of cell *A* is near enough to excite cell *B* and repeatedly or persistently takes part in firing it, some growth process or metabolic change takes place in one or both cells such that *A*'s efficiency, as one of the cells firing *B*, is increased."

**Why:** Hebb's actual rule is explicitly directional (A fires B), whereas the Shatz paraphrase is symmetric. The story's Act XIII tightening — "Hebbian plasticity is not contagious by proximity, it is contagious by co-firing" — correctly preserves the co-occurrence requirement.

**How to apply:** When attributing the "fire/wire" phrase in any new copy, credit Shatz 1992 alongside Hebb 1949, or rephrase to Hebb's own directional language. Worth adding Shatz to the bibliography if the phrase is kept prominent.
