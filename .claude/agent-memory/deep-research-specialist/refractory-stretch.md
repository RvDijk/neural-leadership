**The biology**: Neuronal refractory periods are very short:
- Absolute refractory: ~1-2 ms (Na+ channels inactivated, no firing possible)
- Relative refractory: ~3-4 ms (higher threshold required)

**The story's use (Act IX)**: Treats "refractory period" as a window of minutes-to-hours during which an apology lands wrong. This is metaphorical stretching, not biology.

**Better anchors for the same narrative point:**
- **Amygdala reactivity / hot vs cold states**: LeDoux, J. (1996). *The Emotional Brain.* — supports the "threat-state apology confirms threat" claim.
- **Window of tolerance / polyvagal**: Porges, S. W. (2011). *The Polyvagal Theory.* — supports timing of safe re-engagement (clinical, less peer-reviewed).
- **Emotion regulation / cognitive reappraisal**: Gross, J. J. (1998). The emerging field of emotion regulation. *Review of General Psychology*, 2(3), 271-299.

**Why:** A domain expert in neuroscience reading "refractory period" applied to a multi-hour apology window will wince. The Cross & Parker 2004 citation in the act doesn't establish the mechanism either — it's organizational-network methodology.

**How to apply:** If Act IX is revised, consider renaming to "the repair window" or "the recovery interval," and anchor the mechanism to affective neuroscience (amygdala / autonomic recovery) rather than ion-channel refractory dynamics. The narrative point — timing matters, too-soon backfires, too-late hardens — survives intact.
